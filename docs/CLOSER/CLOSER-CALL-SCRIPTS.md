# Section 1 — Lis Pendens (Just Filed, 4-12 Weeks to Auction)

**Situation handed over by Kareem:**
- Foreclosure case just filed in Common Pleas Court
- Sheriff sale is 4-12 weeks away (no auction date set yet typically)
- Owner has the most time + most options
- Common state: shame / denial / suspicion

**Your tone:** Calm advisor. Time is on their side. You're walking them through math, not closing them under pressure.

## 1.1 LP Condition Intake (10 minutes, 14 points)

After the opener, transition directly into the property walkthrough:

> "Great. So Kareem mentioned the property's at [address] — let me walk through a few things about the house so I can put together accurate numbers. I'll go through this fairly quick — about 14 things — and you tell me what you know, and where you don't know it's totally fine to say so."

Run through these in order (you ask, they answer, you type into REISimpli):

1. **Year built** — confirm the Zillow data
2. **Square footage** — confirm
3. **Bedrooms / bathrooms**
4. **Basement** — finished / unfinished / none / crawl space
5. **Roof age** — when last replaced? Any leaks?
6. **HVAC** — gas / electric / heat pump? When last serviced?
7. **Plumbing** — copper / PEX / galvanized? Any known leaks?
8. **Electric** — breakers or fuses? Service updated?
9. **Kitchen** — original / partial update / full reno? How old?
10. **Bathrooms** — original / partial / updated?
11. **Flooring** — carpet / hardwood / LVP? Condition?
12. **Windows** — original / replaced? Single or double pane?
13. **Foundation** — any cracks, water issues, settling?
14. **Anything else you've been meaning to fix but couldn't?** ← this is the empathy unlock; let them talk

**Listening for:** the gap between Zillow's beds/baths/sqft and reality. Roof + HVAC are the two biggest rehab line items — get specific numbers if possible ("about 5 years ago" beats "I think it's old").

## 1.2 LP ARV Anchor (Two-Bucket frame)

After condition intake, set the comp anchor BEFORE quoting an offer. This is the "show your work" move that builds trust faster than anything else.

> "Okay [first_name], based on what you've shared, let me walk through how I'm thinking about this. I pulled five recent sales within about a half-mile of you over the last six months — homes similar to yours in beds, baths, square footage, and year built. The range was [low ARV] on the low end to [high ARV] on the high end. The most comparable ones — same condition tier as your home if it were fully renovated — averaged around [target ARV]. That's the 'fully fixed' market value — what your house would sell for if a flipper put $40-60K into it and put it back on the MLS.
>
> Make sense so far?"

**What's happening:**
- You're showing them you've done real homework (no winging it)
- You name the ARV as the *fixed* value, not the *as-is* value — this primes the gap that justifies your offer
- "Make sense so far?" — micro-check-in; gets them to nod and stay engaged

**The Two-Bucket model** (used internally to pick which ARV bucket to anchor):
- **Disclosed-state bucket (Ohio is disclosed):** use MLS solds within 0.5 mi, last 6 months, same beds/baths +/- 1, same sqft +/- 20%. Avg of top 3 comparables → ARV.
- **Same-condition tier filter:** don't comp a flip-finished house against your as-is unless you adjust. Use the 4-tier rehab system to estimate where their house sits today.

If they push back on the ARV — *"That seems low, Zillow says it's worth $X"* — see Section 5 objection handlers.

## 1.3 LP Offer Math (the radical-transparency walk)

Now you walk them through HOW we get from ARV to our offer. **Show every line. Don't hide the math.**

> "Okay so here's the math. I'll show you every line — that way you see exactly what we work with and you can decide if it's fair for your situation.
>
> Starting point: the [target ARV] value if your house were fully renovated and on the MLS.
>
> From that I subtract a few things:
>
> **Rehab estimate** — based on what you told me about [roof / HVAC / kitchen / whatever], my contractors run about [Tier X price — see rehab estimator]. Could be a little less, could be a little more, but that's the realistic budget to bring it to MLS-ready condition.
>
> **Resale costs** — when we resell after the rehab, we pay agent commissions, title fees, holding costs while it's on the market, utilities, taxes during the hold. That runs about 10% of the ARV — call it [10% of ARV].
>
> **Our profit margin** — this is the part most folks don't get told. We need to make about 10% on the deal for it to be worth us taking the risk on the rehab. So that's another [10% of ARV].
>
> Add those up, subtract from ARV, and our offer comes in around **[offer number]**."

**Why this works:**
- They see EVERY line. Nothing hidden.
- They understand the offer isn't arbitrary — it's math.
- They can negotiate ANY line (rehab if you over-budgeted, profit if they want to push) and you have room to flex.
- "Most folks don't get told" — the radical-transparency phrase. Sellers remember the rep who explained the math.

## 1.4 LP Offer Presentation + Anchor

Don't just throw out the number. Frame it.

> "Now — I want to be straight with you. The number I just walked you to is what works for us at a fair-deal level. If we landed there, you'd walk away with [offer minus mortgage payoff if known], we'd close on your timeline (usually 7-14 days), no agent fees, no inspections, no repairs needed from your end.
>
> Now compare that to the foreclosure path: if the sheriff sale happens and the bank takes it back, you typically walk away with $0 — banks recoup, you don't get equity returned. So even if our offer is below what Zillow shows, the comparison isn't [offer] vs [Zillow] — it's [offer] vs **probably zero**.
>
> Where does that land for you?"

**What's happening:**
- "Fair-deal level" — you've signaled there's flex without giving it away
- You named the alternative: $0 at sheriff sale. **This is the load-bearing comparison.**
- "Where does that land for you?" — open-ended invitation; you wait in silence

## 1.6 LP × Psychological State — How to Adjust the Whole Call

Lis Pendens is the **lowest time-pressure** distress type — 4-12 weeks of runway. Your job is to use that time as leverage to build trust + lean them toward the cash-sale option without ever pressing. The state determines HOW you lean.

### LP × Denial — "The bank's working with me"
**Likelihood:** Very common (~40% of LP leads).
**What they're really saying:** "I haven't accepted this is real yet."

**How to lean (without confronting denial):**
- **Don't** argue or cite data showing 90% of loan mods fail. That triggers shame.
- **Do** parallel-track: "Keep the bank conversation going — that's smart. Most folks who land okay run two tracks: the bank track AND a backup. Let me show you what the backup looks like financially, so you have it in your pocket."
- Then walk through your full math (Section 1.3) AS the backup, not as the primary.
- **Lean-to-sell move:** After presenting the offer, say *"If the bank comes through with a workable mod in the next 30 days, you tear up this contract — no harm done. If they don't, you've already got the math done. That's the only reason I'd send the contract today — to give you both paths."*
- **Close path:** Send the contract on a "no harm, tear it up" basis. Get them signing while keeping the bank fantasy alive. Many LP+Denial sellers sign within 14 days when the bank stalls.

**Walk-away signal:** if they refuse to even look at the contract because "the mod is definitely coming through" — schedule a 30-day recycle and document. Don't push.

### LP × Shame — "I should've handled this better"
**Likelihood:** Common (~25% of LP leads).
**What they're really saying:** "I'm embarrassed and I'm protecting my dignity."

**How to lean:**
- Tonality goes **slower and lower** on every sentence.
- **Open with normalization:** "Foreclosure happens to thousands of Ohio homeowners every year. Job changes, medical, divorce, family. The only thing that separates outcomes is whether someone walks them through the options. No judgment from my end."
- During condition intake: when they say *"I should've fixed the roof three years ago,"* respond with *"I get it — life happens, money gets pulled in 12 directions. Let's just put a number on it for the math."* Never validate the self-judgment.
- **Lean-to-sell move:** Frame the cash sale as a clean exit that restores their dignity. "Here's the thing — a cash sale closes quietly at the title company, you walk away with money in your pocket, and three months from now nobody knows the details. That's a clean restart."
- **Close path:** Assumptive close ("when do you want to close?"). Avoid take-aways with shame — they trigger withdrawal.

**Walk-away signal:** They become non-responsive or hostile mid-call. Back off. Send a written follow-up the next day. Recycle in 7 days.

### LP × Suspicion — "Are you a wholesaler? Where'd you get my info?"
**Likelihood:** Common (~30% of LP leads).
**What they're really saying:** "I've been called 30 times by scammers and I can't tell who's real."

**How to lean:**
- **Stop the natural sales tonality.** Be informational, procedural, almost boring.
- **Verify everything verifiable:** "Wright Home Offer, [city] Ohio. Aaron Leddy owns it — he's a licensed Ohio Realtor, license number [#] on our website. We close at title companies, not at your house. Earnest money goes to the title company escrow, not to us. Contracts use Ohio standard forms — any attorney in Ohio can review them."
- During the math walk: pause more, let them ask questions. Suspicion thaws via patience, not eloquence.
- **Lean-to-sell move:** Offer to text them references before continuing. "Want me to text you the names + numbers of two folks we've closed with in Franklin County in the last 90 days? They'll tell you what working with us was actually like." 9 times out of 10 they say no, but the offer alone moves them from -10 to +5 on trust.
- **Close path:** Assumptive, with extra friction-removal — *"I'll send the contract via DocuSign, you can have anyone review it before signing, no pressure on speed."*

**Walk-away signal:** Hostility escalates instead of decreases. Recycle in 14 days — sometimes they call back after burning out on other "buyers" who confirmed their suspicion.

### LP × Magical Thinking — "My brother's going to give me the money"
**Likelihood:** Less common (~10%).
**What they're really saying:** "I'm holding onto a rescue plan because I can't face the alternative."

**How to lean:**
- **Don't kill the hope.** Validate it briefly: "That's great, I hope it comes through."
- **Then parallel-track:** "While you're waiting on him, let me walk through the backup math so if the timing slips you're not scrambling. Five minutes."
- During offer presentation: frame as insurance, not primary. *"This is the 'just in case' number. If your brother comes through, tear up the contract."*
- **Lean-to-sell move:** Set a soft deadline tied to the auction timeline. "If we're 21 days from a sheriff sale and the brother thing hasn't materialized, that's the point where the math gets tight on closing in time. Want me to check in then to see where things stand?"
- **Close path:** Send contract anyway, label it backup. They sign it 30-50% of the time when the rescue plan dissolves.

### LP × Decision Fatigue — "I just want this to go away"
**Likelihood:** ~15%.
**What they're really saying:** "My bandwidth is gone, please make it simple."

**How to lean:**
- **Reduce the call to ONE decision.** "I'm going to send you a contract today. The only thing you need to decide is: does the number work? Three lines: price, closing date, earnest money. That's it. Everything else is the title company's job."
- Skip the full math walk if their fatigue is severe. They'll trust the summary.
- **Lean-to-sell move:** *"Here's what closing looks like: you sign one document, the title company handles the rest, money hits your account in two weeks. That's the whole thing."*
- **Close path:** Assumptive + concrete next step. *"I'll email the contract in 30 minutes. Sign it tonight or tomorrow morning. Sound okay?"*

## 1.5 LP Close — Three Outcomes

### Outcome A — They say "Yes, let's do it"

> "Great. Here's what happens next. I'll send you a purchase agreement today — it'll come to your email. It's standard Ohio real estate contract, two pages, nothing weird. You sign it and send it back, we put up earnest money — $500 — into title, and we get the title company started on the closing.
>
> I'm thinking we could close [day, ~10 business days out]. Does that timeline work, or do you need it faster or slower?"

Lock the closing date. Confirm email address. Send the contract within 60 minutes of hanging up — speed is trust.

### Outcome B — They want to think about it

> "Totally fair. This is a big decision. Here's what I'd offer: I'll send the purchase agreement over today anyway — no obligation, just so you can look at the numbers in writing and read the contract on your own schedule. I'll follow up [day after tomorrow] at [time] — just one call, no pressure. If you're a yes by then, great. If you're not, you tell me and I close the file. That fair?"

**Speed of follow-up matters.** 48h is the sweet spot — long enough for them to think, short enough that the urgency of their distress doesn't fade.

### Outcome C — They want to negotiate the number

> "Okay, talk to me. Where do you need the number to be, and why?"

**Listen to the WHY before responding.** Their why tells you which line in your math you can flex on:
- *"I owe $X on the mortgage"* → you can flex profit slightly to clear their payoff + give them moving money
- *"My brother told me the house is worth $X"* → recomp work needed; either show your comps or revisit ARV
- *"I need at least $Y to start over"* → motivation-aligned negotiation; sometimes a higher offer is justified if condition was overestimated

**Never flex more than ~5% of ARV without a real reason.** Set your walk-away floor before the call (see prep checklist) and honor it.

## 2.6 SS × Psychological State — Handle Each State With Time Pressure In Mind

Sheriff Sale is **high time pressure**, 14-30 days to auction. The clock is real and every state needs to be handled with that constraint front-of-mind. The biggest closer mistake: applying LP-style patience to an SS lead. They don't have the time. Your job: lean hard toward the cash sale **while staying respectful** — this is where rep skill matters most.

### SS × Denial — "The bank's still working with me, this won't go to sale" ⚠️ HIGHEST STAKES INTERSECTION
**Likelihood:** Very common (~50% of SS leads). The most dangerous combination — the auction is in 14-30 days and they're refusing to act.
**What they're really saying:** "If I act, I'm admitting it's real. As long as I keep believing, it doesn't have to be real."

**How to lean (this is the playbook for this combination):**

**Phase 1 — Validate the denial briefly (60 seconds, no more):**
> "Okay, I hear you. Sometimes banks do come through right at the end — I've seen it happen. So let me ask: what's the bank told you specifically? Are they giving you a written modification offer, or are they just saying 'we're working on it'?"

Listen carefully. Two outcomes:
- **They have a written offer with a closing date** → genuinely high probability mod. Parallel-track only (see LP+Denial above). Light touch.
- **They have "we're working on it" verbal** → 90% chance the mod won't materialize before auction. Move to Phase 2.

**Phase 2 — Reality-anchor without confrontation:**
> "Got it. Look, I want to be straight with you because the calendar matters here. Banks legally don't have to honor verbal mod conversations. They can still complete the foreclosure even while saying they're 'working on it' — and we see that happen probably 8 out of 10 times when there's no signed mod paperwork by 21 days before sale. I'm not telling you this to be the bad-news guy — I'm telling you because you deserve to know what the runway actually looks like."

**Tonality:** peer-to-peer, slow, almost apologetic. You're not pitching. You're informing.

**Phase 3 — The two-track math:**
> "Here's what I'd offer. Two tracks running at the same time.
>
> **Track 1**: You keep pushing the bank — written mod with signatures or nothing. Set a deadline in your head: by day 21 before sale, if you don't have signed mod paperwork, the bank's not coming through.
>
> **Track 2**: We have a backup contract sitting at the title company. Earnest money goes in. Title work starts. You don't have to sign anything that locks you in today — let me send the contract, you read it, we keep talking. If your bank delivers signed paperwork in time, you tear it up, no harm done. If they don't, you're not scrambling 7 days before the sheriff sale.
>
> Track 2 is the move that lets you walk away with money in either scenario. Track 1 alone is a coin flip with the bank holding the coin."

**Phase 4 — The lean-to-sell anchor:**
After the math walk, present the offer with this framing:
> "If we close at [offer], you walk away with [equity number] and the house is off your plate. Compare that to the auction outcome where the bank takes everything and you net $0. Even if Track 1 succeeds and you keep the house, a year from now you're still paying that mortgage on a depreciating asset in a market that's slowing. Track 2 ends the conversation cleanly. Where does that land for you?"

**Close path:**
- Assumptive close on the contract: "I'll send the contract today, you read it tomorrow, we talk again Friday. Sound good?"
- **Don't** push for a same-day signature. Their denial system will reject it.
- **Do** get the contract drafted and into their email. The act of receiving it shifts the psychology — they're now reviewing a real document, not a hypothetical.

**Walk-away signal:** They explicitly refuse to receive the contract or block the email. At 21 days before sale, mark dead. At 14 days before sale, one final urgent VM, then dead.

### SS × Decision Fatigue — "I just need this to be over"
**Likelihood:** ~25%.
**What they're really saying:** "I've been making hard calls for weeks. My bandwidth is gone."

**How to lean:**
- **Make every step smaller than the last.** "I'm sending one document today. You read three lines: price, closing date, earnest money. Sign if it works. That's the whole decision today."
- Skip optional condition-intake questions if they're answering in monosyllables. Speed becomes kindness here.
- **Lean-to-sell:** *"In 10 business days, this is over — at the title company, you sign one set of papers, money hits your account, you move on. That's the simplest path through any of this."*
- **Close path:** Assumptive + ultra-concrete. Send DocuSign within 30 min, follow up in 24 hours.

**Walk-away signal:** unresponsive to 2 follow-ups. Don't keep pressing — set a 7-day recycle.

### SS × Magical Thinking — "My family is going to come up with the money"
**Likelihood:** ~15%.
**What they're really saying:** "There's a last-minute rescue I'm holding onto."

**How to lean:**
- **Set the auction date as the truth-teller.** "When do they say the money will be available?"
- If their answer is *before* the auction by at least 14 days → parallel-track (see LP+Magical). Send contract as backup.
- If their answer is *after* the auction or vague → reality-anchor:
> "Okay — I'm rooting for that to come through. The thing is, the sheriff sale doesn't pause for family money. If the auction happens before they wire it, the bank takes the property and your family loses both the house and the money they would've put in. Let me at least send the backup contract so they don't lose their loan if the timing slips."

**Close path:** Backup contract framing. Get them signing 30-40% of the time when the magical money fails to materialize on time.

### SS × Suspicion — "Where'd you get my info, who are you really?"
**Likelihood:** ~15%, but spikes for first-time foreclosures.
**What they're really saying:** "Half the people calling me about this are scammers."

**How to lean:**
- **Front-load verification before any math.** "Wright Home Offer, [city] Ohio, licensed Realtor [Aaron's #]. We close at title companies. You can have any attorney review the contract. I'll text you proof before we go further."
- **Lean-to-sell:** The trust-builder for suspicious SS leads is the auction-date math itself. Show them the foreclosure docket, the auction listing. Public record is your friend here. "I'm not making this up — your case number is [case#], your sheriff sale is set for [date]. You can verify everything I just said in 30 seconds on the county website."
- **Close path:** Slow contract send, extra friction-removal. *"DocuSign, any attorney can review, no pressure on timing for at least 48 hours."*

### SS × Shame — "I let this happen, I can't believe it got here"
**Likelihood:** ~20%.
**What they're really saying:** "I'm protecting my dignity, please don't make it worse."

**How to lean:**
- Tonality drops two notches lower than your default.
- Normalize the situation: "Foreclosure happens to thousands. You're not failing — you're navigating."
- **Lean-to-sell:** Frame the cash sale as a clean restart, not a defeat. *"Here's the thing — selling now, on your terms, with money in your pocket, is the strongest move available. Letting it go to auction is the move where the bank wins. You walking away with [equity number] is YOU winning a hard situation."*
- **Close path:** Assumptive close. Avoid take-away with shame-state leads.

### SS × Learned Helplessness — "Whatever happens, happens, just take it"
**Likelihood:** ~10%, but disproportionately the most dangerous.
**What they're really saying:** "I've stopped advocating for myself."

**This is the state where the closer protects the seller from themselves:**

> "Hold on a second [first_name]. I want to slow down. You're agreeing to my offer pretty quickly and I want to make sure you actually feel okay with the number, not just that you're tired. The number I gave you is fair, but I want to make sure three months from now you don't look back and feel like you got rushed. Is the number something you can feel okay with, or are you saying yes because you just want this to be done?"

**You may talk them OUT of accepting too fast.** They will remember it. It's the most expensive trust-builder you'll ever pay for, but it pays back in referrals and future deals. Don't take advantage of helplessness — even when it would close the deal today.

**Close path:** Assumptive only when they show actual conviction. Verify the yes is real. Send contract with a 48-hour read window even if they want to sign faster.

## 2.5 SS Close — Three Outcomes

### Outcome A — They say yes

Same contract + earnest money flow as LP (see 1.5.A), but compressed timeline:
- Contract emailed within 30 minutes (not 60)
- Earnest money up within 24 hours
- Title work starts same day
- Closing date 7-10 business days

### Outcome B — They want to think

This is harder than LP. The auction clock matters.

> "I hear you, and I get it. The only thing I'll be straight with you about: if you want to think about it for a week, the auction calendar doesn't pause. I'll send you the purchase agreement today so you can read it, and I'll follow up tomorrow — not [day after tomorrow], tomorrow — just to check in. If you're still thinking, we'll do another check-in. But I want to be honest with you: if we get to [N-7] days out, we're past the point where we can close in time. So we'd have to make a call before then."

Document the conversation in REISimpli + set callback for the next day.

### Outcome C — They want to negotiate

Same as LP 1.5.C, but **less room to flex** because the timeline cost is real (rush title fees, contractor mobilization, etc.). Walk-away floor is ~3% of ARV flex, not 5%.

### Outcome D — They want to attempt other options first

> "Totally fair. Here's what I'd ask: I'll send you the contract anyway, no obligation. If your other plans come through, you tear it up and I move on, no hard feelings. If they don't come through, you've got a backup that's already drafted — you sign and we move fast. That cover your bases?"

This is the **parallel-track frame** from the ISA framework, applied at close-time. Don't kill their hope; provide insurance.

# Section 3 — Redemption Window (Sheriff Sale Done, 30-Day Equity Window)

**Situation handed over by Kareem:**
- Sheriff sale has happened — third party (or bank) is the auction winner
- Owner has Ohio's statutory ~28-day redemption window to repurchase
- Hot deadline; sometimes only 5-7 days left when we reach them
- Common state: decision fatigue / shame / learned helplessness

**Your tone:** Empathetic, fast, action-oriented. They're past the point where comfort matters — they need a clear path NOW.

## 3.1 RW Critical Pre-Call Prep

Before dialing:
- [ ] Confirm exact redemption deadline (from court docket or Common Pleas record)
- [ ] Calculate redemption price = auction sale price + sheriff fees + accrued interest
- [ ] Know IF the auction price was less than fair-market value (this is when redemption-window deals work)
- [ ] **If redemption price > ARV - rehab - costs, walk away. There's no deal.** Tell Kareem to recycle.

## 3.2 RW Opener

> "Hi, is this [first_name]? Danielle from Wright Home Offer. Kareem mentioned the sheriff sale on [address] happened on [date]. I want to be straight with you — there's a window, sometimes called redemption, where Ohio law lets you buy your house back from the auction winner if you can come up with the money in time. That window closes [date].
>
> I work with folks in this situation pretty often. I'm not selling you anything in the next ten minutes — I'm going to walk through whether the math works for you to either redeem and resell OR for us to step in and help you redeem. Got 10 minutes?"

**What's different:**
- You explain the redemption window in 2 sentences (most owners don't fully understand it)
- "Buy your house back" — accurate, dignifying language
- "Either redeem and resell OR for us to step in" — two paths offered upfront

## 3.3 RW Math Walk

The math is different in RW. It's not ARV minus rehab minus profit. It's redemption-price-funded-by-Wright-Home-Offer with a structured equity share.

> "Here's the math. The auction sold at [auction price]. The sheriff added about [fees] in fees, plus interest from the sale date — call it [redemption_total]. That's what someone has to come up with by [deadline] to redeem.
>
> If you could come up with that money, you could redeem and then list the house, sell it for [ARV - rehab estimate - 10% costs = realistic resale net], and pocket the difference: about [equity remaining].
>
> But — most folks can't come up with [redemption_total] in [N] days. That's where we sometimes step in. We bring the cash to redeem on your behalf, you assign us the right to title at closing, and we work out a split of the equity that comes out. Typical structure: we recover our redemption cash plus [10-20%] of the equity, you keep the rest. So you'd walk away with roughly [estimated walk-away] instead of $0.
>
> Make sense so far?"

This is more complex than LP/SS — slow down. Don't rush through the math.

## 3.5 RW × Psychological State — Empathy + Speed in Equal Measure

Redemption Window is **extreme time pressure** (often 5-7 days left) AND comes attached to a recent loss event (sheriff sale happened, dignity took a hit). Most leads here have already been through SS+Denial or SS+Magical and lost — they're in helplessness or shame now. Your job: fast empathy, fast math, fast contract.

### RW × Learned Helplessness — "It's already gone, whatever"
**Likelihood:** Very common (~45% of RW leads). The default state by the time someone reaches RW.
**What they're really saying:** "Trying didn't work last time. Why would it work now?"

**How to lean:**
- **Re-engage their advocacy WITHOUT requiring them to fight.** "I'm not asking you to fight the sale or argue with the bank. I'm asking you to spend 10 minutes seeing if there's any money left for you in this. There might be. There might not be. Worth 10 minutes to know."
- During math walk: name the dollar amount they could walk away with **in concrete language**. "If we run this redemption assignment, you'd walk away with approximately $14,000. That's $14,000 you'd have instead of $0 if we let the window close."
- **Lean-to-sell:** $14,000 vs $0 is the entire frame. They will hear the number through the helplessness fog if you stay on it.
- **Close path:** Assumptive + fast. "I'm sending the redemption-assignment contract now. Sign it tonight. Title needs to start tomorrow. Sound okay?"

**Walk-away signal:** They don't respond to the contract within 24 hours. The window closes regardless. Mark dead, no recycle (redemption doesn't recycle — once the window closes, it's gone).

### RW × Decision Fatigue — "I can't process anything else"
**Likelihood:** ~30%.
**What they're really saying:** "Everything's been a fire drill for weeks."

**How to lean:**
- **Single-decision frame:** "One document. Three lines: redemption price, my fee, what you walk away with. Sign or don't. That's the only decision today."
- Skip optional questions entirely. Get to the math, get to the contract.
- **Lean-to-sell:** *"In 7 business days, your name is off the property and there's money in your account. That's the path through this."*
- **Close path:** Send within 30 min. Follow up within 24h. Title same day.

### RW × Shame — "I should've sold before the auction"
**Likelihood:** ~20%.
**What they're really saying:** "I made the wrong call before. I can't tell if I'm making the wrong call again."

**How to lean:**
- **Normalize:** "Hindsight makes the SS decision look obvious. In real time, with a bank that was 'working with you,' it never feels obvious. Tons of folks land here. The good news: there's still money on the table, if we move now."
- **Lean-to-sell:** Frame redemption assignment as the second-chance move. *"This is the move that recovers some of what would've been the equity. Not all of it — but a real number. Better than nothing, and you didn't make this happen, you're responding to it."*
- **Close path:** Assumptive. Don't take-away with shame.

### RW × Suspicion — "How is this even legal?"
**Likelihood:** ~15%. Redemption assignment is unfamiliar to most homeowners; suspicion is natural.
**What they're really saying:** "This sounds too good to be true."

**How to lean:**
- **Educational first, sales second.** Walk them through Ohio's ORC §2329.33 redemption statute briefly. "Ohio law gives the property owner ~28 days after a sheriff sale to redeem the property by paying the auction price + fees. That right is yours. You can also legally assign that right to another buyer (us) in exchange for them bringing the redemption money and sharing the equity that comes out. It's a real, established structure — title companies handle dozens of these per year in Ohio."
- **Verify your structure:** "The redemption funds go through the title company, not through me. The court signs off on the redemption. Everything is documented and recorded."
- **Lean-to-sell:** *"The most suspicious thing about this is that most homeowners don't know it exists. The banks and auction buyers don't advertise it because they don't want you to recover any equity. We're the ones telling you it's available."*
- **Close path:** Send contract + offer to walk through any clause with their attorney. Suspicion-types respond well to attorney involvement.

### RW × Grief (rare but happens — when probate + foreclosure overlap)
**Likelihood:** <5% but worth noting.
**What they're really saying:** "I'm dealing with a death AND a foreclosure."

**How to lean:**
- Slow down. Acknowledge the loss. "Before anything else — I'm sorry about [decedent]. I know dealing with all this on top of a loss is a lot."
- Move at probate speed even though the calendar says RW speed. Sometimes the math doesn't work in time and the right answer is to let the window close.
- **If the math works:** present quickly, send contract, but pair it with "if your family isn't ready to make this call this week, that's okay — the redemption window will close. We won't push."
- **Close path:** Their call. If they sign, move fast. If they don't, document and don't push.

## 3.4 RW Close — Two Outcomes

### Outcome A — Math works, they're in

Same contract flow, but it's a redemption-assignment contract, not a standard purchase agreement. Talk to title first. Speed is everything.

> "Great. I'm going to send you a redemption-assignment contract today. It's not a normal purchase agreement — it's an agreement that says I bring the redemption money, you assign me the right to the property at closing, and we split the equity as we just discussed. **Be honest about no-deal scenarios.** Sellers remember the rep who told them "this isn't worth it for you" — that referral or future deal is worth more than the wasted hour pushing a bad close.

# Section 4 — Probate (Deceased Owner, 6-9 Month Estate Runway)

**Situation handed over by Kareem:**
- Probate case filed in Ohio probate court
- Owner of record (decedent) has passed away
- Fiduciary (executor / administrator / heir) is the decision-maker
- Estate is typically 6-9 months from filing to close
- Common state: grief / decision fatigue / fear of judgment

**Your tone:** Quiet, respectful, patient. This is not a transactional call. The relationship matters more than the immediate close.

## 4.1 Probate Pre-Call Prep (CRITICAL)

Before dialing:
- [ ] Read the obituary (the URL is in REISimpli + deep-prospect PDF)
- [ ] Note: date of death, family members named, decedent's age, cause if disclosed
- [ ] Note the fiduciary's relationship to decedent (spouse / child / sibling / executor)
- [ ] Know the property address vs. the fiduciary's mailing address (often the fiduciary lives elsewhere)
- [ ] Pull the comp report
- [ ] Read the deep-prospect PDF — it has heir tree, DM confidence, full research context

**Skip none of this. The pre-call prep is the difference between a respectful probate call and a tone-deaf one.**

## 4.2 Probate Opener — different from LP/SS/RW

You DO NOT lead with the property. You lead with empathy.

> "Hi, is this [first_name]? Hi [first_name], this is Danielle from Wright Home Offer — Kareem mentioned we'd be talking today. Before anything else, I wanted to say — I'm really sorry about [decedent first name]. I know this is a hard season and the legal stuff on top of it makes it harder.
>
> I'm not going to pressure you on anything today. Kareem mentioned the estate includes the property on [address] — when it's the right time, I'd love to walk through what we'd offer and how the process works, but I want to be respectful of where you're at. How are things going on your end?"

**What's happening:**
- Decedent named by first name — humanizing
- "Hard season" — language of loss, not transaction
- "Not going to pressure you" — explicit
- "How are things going on your end?" — invitation, not interrogation

**Wait in silence. Let them talk.** This is the call where you listen 70%, speak 30%.

## 4.3 Probate Condition Intake — same 14 points, but slower

Run the 14-point intake from Section 1.1, but at half-pace. Pause longer between questions. Let them share stories ("My dad bought this house in 1972...") — those stories are equity for the relationship.

## 4.4 Probate ARV Anchor + Offer

The math is the same Two-Bucket + transparency walk as LP. But the framing changes:

> "Okay [first_name], let me walk you through the numbers. I want to be respectful of what the house means to your family — and I also want to be honest about what the market is. Both can be true.
>
> [ARV anchor — same as Section 1.2]
>
> [Math walk — same as Section 1.3]
>
> Where this lands: our offer would be around **[offer]**. We close at the title company in Columbus, on your timeline — sometimes families want to wait until [Christmas / a grandkid's wedding / the year-end] before clearing out the house. We work around that. If your family wants to take out personal belongings before closing, we give you as much time as you need.
>
> No pressure on today's call — but tell me what you're thinking."

**The closing-on-your-timeline frame is the probate-specific magic.** Probate families are often weeks or months from emotionally being ready to clear the house. Saying "we wait" instead of "we close in 14 days" is what separates Wright Home Offer from every wholesaler hammering them.

## 4.6 Probate × Psychological State — Patience Is the Multiplier

Probate has **no time pressure** (6-9 month estate runway). Your job is the OPPOSITE of SS/RW: build relationship over multiple touches, lean to sell only when the family is ready. Probate states stack — most leads show 2-3 states at once.

### Probate × Grief — "It's only been a few weeks"
**Likelihood:** Very common (~50% of probate leads), especially in the first 30-60 days after filing.
**What they're really saying:** "I'm not ready to think of this as a transaction."

**How to lean (this is the patience play):**
- **Back off the math entirely on the first call.** Don't anchor an offer. Don't run condition intake.
- **Just listen.** "How is the family doing?" Then wait. Let them talk about the decedent if they want to.
- **Soft pre-frame for future calls:** "I'm going to stop here today. I don't want to pressure you on any of the property stuff. When the family's ready — could be 30 days, could be 6 months — I'd love to come back and walk through the numbers. No timeline from me. Sound okay?"
- **Lean-to-sell move (long-game):** End with *"Keep my number. If anything changes — questions about the probate process, questions about what the house might be worth — text or call. No pressure to do anything with it."*
- **Close path:** No close today. Set a 30-45 day recycle in REISimpli with note `Probate-Grief-Recycle`.

**Why this wins:** The probate leads who turn into contracts at month 4-6 remember EXACTLY which "buyer" backed off in month 1. The patience compounds.

### Probate × Decision Fatigue — "There's so much paperwork already"
**Likelihood:** ~30%, peaks at week 6-12 of probate.
**What they're really saying:** "I've been dealing with court forms for weeks. The thought of more documents exhausts me."

**How to lean:**
- **Frame your transaction as the OPPOSITE of probate paperwork.** "Look, probate is a paperwork nightmare — I've watched dozens of families go through it. Here's the good news on our side: we close at the title company, one set of forms, the title attorney handles probate-specific clauses, and you sign once. Way less paperwork than the probate process itself."
- During the 14-point intake, be efficient. Don't ask anything you can look up.
- **Lean-to-sell:** *"Closing the sale actually CLOSES the estate's biggest asset. Once the house is sold, distribution to heirs becomes much simpler — cash splits clean, real estate splits messy. The cash sale is sometimes the move that lets you finally close out the probate."*
- **Close path:** Assumptive close on the timeline. *"What closing date works for the family? Some families want fast — wrap it up — some want to wait for [holiday]. We work around it."*

### Probate × Fear of Judgment — "Don't tell anyone about this"
**Likelihood:** ~20%, especially with elder decedents in tight communities.
**What they're really saying:** "Family politics. Neighbors talking. I want this quiet."

**How to lean:**
- **Explicit confidentiality language:** "Everything we discuss is private. No signs go up at the property. Closing happens at the title company in [city], not at the house. The deed records publicly eventually — that's true for any sale — but no one's hunting county records about your family. As far as anyone outside the family knows, the house just changed owners."
- **Lean-to-sell:** *"Privacy and speed work together here. A cash sale closes in 14-30 days, quietly. Listing on the MLS means showings, open houses, signs, and 60-90 days of neighbors asking questions. Cash sale is the quiet path."*
- **Close path:** Assumptive. Reiterate privacy on every touch.

### Probate × Magical Thinking — "My brother might buy it from the estate"
**Likelihood:** ~15%.
**What they're really saying:** "A family member said they'd buy it but nothing's in writing."

**How to lean:**
- **Don't kill the hope.** "That's great if it works. I've seen those work out and I've seen them stall out."
- **Then parallel-track:** "While that conversation happens, let me put numbers on paper for the backup. That way the family has hard data — what we'd offer, what a Realtor estimates the MLS would net minus commissions and time. If your brother sees what the real-money options are, he can either match it or step back, and you've got a backup either way."
- **Lean-to-sell:** Numbers force the family conversation. *"Sometimes the brother offer is generous because no one's looked at the real-world numbers yet. Once we put a number on the table, the family conversation changes — either he matches and we step back, or he steps back and we move forward. Either way, you're not stuck waiting on a maybe."*
- **Close path:** Backup contract. Frame as decision-forcing function for the family conversation.

### Probate × Suspicion — "Why are you even calling about this?"
**Likelihood:** ~15%. Often spikes when the fiduciary lives out-of-state and doesn't know Ohio probate is public record.
**What they're really saying:** "How do you know about my dad's death?"

**How to lean:**
- **Front-load the public-records explanation:** "Probate filings are public record in Ohio — same way property records are. When [decedent] filed in [county] probate court, the case became searchable. We're not pulling from anything private. We're an Ohio buyer that works with families in probate situations, helping when it's the right fit."
- **Acknowledge the awkwardness:** "I know it can feel weird to hear from a buyer when you're in the middle of grief. Totally fair reaction. I'm not pressing on anything today — just calling to introduce myself and let you know we're here if and when the family wants to talk about the property."
- **Lean-to-sell:** Don't lean on the first call. Suspicion-types need 2-3 touches before they're ready to talk numbers.
- **Close path:** No close. "Keep my number. When the family's ready, give me a call. No follow-up from me unless you want one."

### Probate × Shame — "We should've had a will / a plan"
**Likelihood:** ~10%. Often shows up when intestate (no will) or when family conflicts are about to surface.
**What they're really saying:** "There's family drama I don't want to talk about."

**How to lean:**
- **Normalize:** "Most families don't have a written plan for any of this. The folks who land okay are the ones who get help walking through the steps. No judgment from my end on the family dynamics — that's between you all."
- **Lean-to-sell:** Frame the cash sale as a clean way to resolve potential family disputes. *"One thing cash sales do well: they convert 'a house we have to figure out' into 'cash we can distribute according to the will or per probate court rules.' Cash is simple. Real estate causes disputes."*
- **Close path:** Slow assumptive. Don't push if family conflict is active — wait it out.

# Section 6 — Closing Tactics + Earnest Money + Contract Flow

## 6.1 When to use the assumptive close

After they've shown buying signals (asking about closing dates, mentioning moving plans, asking how the title company works):

> "Sounds like we're aligned. I'll send the contract over today and we can target [closing date]. Sound good?"

**Don't ask "do you want to do this?"** Ask "when do you want to close?"

## 6.2 When to use the take-away close

When they're stalling, pushing for unrealistic terms, or trying to grind:

> "Look, [first_name] — I want to be honest. I don't think this is going to work for us. The price you're asking for is above what the math supports for me, and I don't want to waste your time stringing this along. Let me suggest you talk to a Realtor — I can refer one — and try the MLS route. If that doesn't pan out, my offer stands at [offer] for the next 14 days. Otherwise, I think we're going to part ways here. Fair?"

**Take-away closes only work when they're genuine.** If you wouldn't actually walk away, don't pretend to. Sellers can smell a fake take-away.

## 6.3 The contract send (60-minute rule)

Within 60 minutes of hanging up on a yes:
- Send the purchase agreement via email (PandaDoc / DocuSign / REISimpli)
- Subject: "[address] — Purchase Agreement from Wright Home Offer"
- Body: "Hi [first_name], as promised — purchase agreement attached. Key terms recap: price [offer], earnest money $500, closing [date]. Read it on your time, ask any questions, sign when ready. — Danielle"
- BCC: yourself + Aaron + REISimpli notification email

Speed of follow-up = trust signal. If you wait until tomorrow to send, the close rate drops by ~30%.

## 6.4 Earnest money

- **Standard amount:** $500
- **Destination:** title company escrow (never your account, never Aaron's account)
- **Timing:** within 24 hours of contract signing
- **Method:** wire or cashier's check to title company

**Title company:** [pick your default — confirm with Aaron]

## 6.5 Closing date negotiation

| Situation | Recommended closing window |
|---|---|
| LP — no auction date yet | 10-14 business days (lets us run title cleanly) |

**Always confirm the closing date THREE TIMES** before hanging up. The TARP rule from the ISA framework still applies.
